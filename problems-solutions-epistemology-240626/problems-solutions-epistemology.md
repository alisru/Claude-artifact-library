# The Problems-Solutions Asymmetry

## The Core Distinction

Problems and solutions are not epistemically equivalent. Problems are objectively visible and verifiable. A housing crisis, a failing bridge, a contaminated water supply can be pointed to, measured, and confirmed independent of any proposed response. Solutions are not like this. A solution is a claim about what will happen if a particular action is taken. That claim sits on a spectrum from near-objective to entirely speculative, and where it sits on that spectrum is determined by scale.

Small solutions are close to objective. Filling a pothole uses a known material, a known method, with a trackable result. The claim is almost entirely verifiable in advance by reference to prior instances. Large solutions are close to speculative. Restructuring housing finance involves causal chains across institutions, time horizons, and human behaviour that cannot be fully modelled before implementation. The claim rests on theory, analogy, and projection rather than direct precedent.

This is not a failure of imagination or political will. It is a structural property of solution-space.

## Trustworthiness as a Compounding Problem

Trustworthiness scales inversely with novelty. A solution that uses known tools in known ways inherits the trust those tools have accumulated. A solution that requires new mechanisms or new combinations of mechanisms has no trust to inherit. Its trust prior is zero by definition.

This would be a manageable constraint if novel solutions were rare. But novel solutions are precisely what large problems demand, because large problems exist in the gap between what existing tools can address and what the problem requires. A problem that existing tools could solve would already be solved. The demand for a solution and the constraint against trusting novel ones are structurally incompatible.

Untrustworthiness is not merely a political liability. It functions as a second-order problem layered on top of the original. A proposed solution that is not trusted must first overcome its own credibility deficit before it can address the problem it was designed for. The larger the solution, the larger that deficit, and the more political and epistemic resources must be spent on legitimation rather than implementation.

## Why Solutions Cannot Flood the Field

Political actors routinely use information saturation as a tactic, generating a volume of claims that overwhelms opposition capacity to refute them. This works for problems because problems are objective claims. Stacking ten verifiable problems produces ten units of verified weight.

Stacking ten contested solutions produces noise. Each solution is independently contestable, and the contestability of each one compounds the perceived unreliability of the whole set. A large volume of proposed solutions does not read as evidence of capacity. It reads as indecision, desperation, or dishonesty, because a confident and correct actor would not need ten proposals to address one problem.

The information environment reinforces this asymmetry. Problems generate engagement because they are concrete and emotionally immediate. Solutions generate scepticism because they are abstract and deferrable. Media and political incentive structures are shaped around this asymmetry. Problems are covered. Solutions are debated, which is not the same thing.

## The Dilution Trap

The asymmetry between trust and novelty produces a predictable policy cycle.

The first solutions proposed for any significant problem are the most trusted ones, built from familiar tools and incremental modifications to existing mechanisms. These solutions inherit high trust but are insufficient by the logic above: if familiar tools were adequate, the problem would not have required a solution. They address symptoms or slow deterioration without resolving the underlying structure.

When incremental solutions visibly fail or produce diminishing returns, slightly less familiar solutions become politically viable. The overton window shifts under pressure from observed failure rather than from argument. This cycle continues through increasingly novel proposals, each one becoming acceptable only after the previous tier has demonstrably exhausted itself.

The formal structure is:

Trusted solutions use known tools.
Known tools were insufficient or the problem would not exist.
Therefore trusted solutions cannot fully solve the problem.
Therefore real solutions only become viable once diluted solutions are exhausted.

This is not a contingent feature of particular political cultures or systems. It follows from the relationship between trust and novelty applied to the structure of problems and solutions.

The corollary is uncomfortable: the faster diluted solutions fail visibly, the faster the window for real solutions opens. Accelerated failure is the mechanism by which genuine political change becomes possible.

## The Rate Threshold

The dilution cycle, though slow, is a self-correcting system. Given sufficient time and sufficiently visible failure, the window for novel solutions opens. This changes when the rate at which new problems are generated exceeds the combined capacity of deployed solutions and solutions in development.

At that threshold, the system moves from slow to structurally deficient. The dilution cycle still operates but cannot keep pace with the incoming load. Each cycle clears one problem tier while two or three new problems accumulate. Novel solutions may find their window, but the stack they are required to address has grown beneath them during the time spent exhausting diluted alternatives.

This condition is qualitatively different from slowness. A slow system eventually corrects. A deficient system is outpaced, and the gap compounds. Critically, a deficient system does not look different from a functioning slow one from the inside. Incremental solutions are still being proposed, debated, and partially implemented. The failure is not visible in any single cycle. It is visible only across cycles, in the growing distance between the rate of problem accumulation and the rate of resolution.

The dark corollary from the previous section applies with greater force here. The faster failure becomes visible, the sooner the window opens. But if the problem rate is high enough, the window opens into a backlog that no single solution, however correct, can clear alone.

## Summary

Problems are objective. Solutions are a trust gradient scaled to novelty. Large solutions require novel thought, novel thought has no trust prior, and the demand for trust and the requirement for novelty are structurally irreconcilable under normal political conditions. Policy cycles through diluted solutions until exhaustion creates the conditions for real ones. When problem generation outpaces that cycle, the system enters structural deficit, which compounds invisibly until the gap becomes a crisis.

This is not a description of political failure. It is a description of how political epistemology works.
