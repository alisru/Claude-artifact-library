# The Problems-Solutions Asymmetry

There's a structural asymmetry between problems and solutions that nobody talks about directly, and it produces most of the dysfunction in political epistemology.

Problems are imminently visible and objectively true.
Solutions are a spectrum from objective to subjective, and that spectrum scales with the size of the solution.

I mean, fix a pothole, that's near-objective. Restructure housing finance, that's almost entirely speculative. The bigger the solution, the further it sits from the objective end, and the further it sits from objective, the less trustworthy it is. And untrustworthiness is itself a problem. So a large solution introduces a second-order problem on top of the original one.

That's not a rhetorical observation, that's the actual mechanism.

---

## Why Solutions Can't Flood the Field

Problems are newsworthy because they're visible, concrete, and objectively verifiable. You can point to them.
Solutions aren't, because they're proposed, speculative, and contested.

The "flood the zone" strategy works for problems because you're stacking objectively true things.
You can't do the same with solutions because solutions are a trust gradient, not a truth gradient.

Flooding the field with solutions just looks like noise, or worse, desperation, because each one is contestable and the more of them you stack the more contested the total picture becomes.

---

## The Catch-22

Here's the actual trap.

Trusted solutions use things people already know.
But if the things people already knew were sufficient, the problem would already be solved.
So trusted solutions can't fully solve the problem, because real solutions require new thought by definition.

I mean, that's not a values argument, that's just logic. The problem exists because existing tools failed to prevent it. A solution built from those same tools is at best a patch, not a fix.

So what happens is a dilution cycle:

The first solutions proposed are the most trusted ones (familiar tools, known mechanisms).
They partially work or visibly fail.
Slightly less familiar solutions get tried.
The cycle continues until diluted solutions can be diluted no further.
At that point, the novel solution becomes less risky than continued failure.

Real solutions only get passed once the dilution space is exhausted.

---

## The Trust-Scale Inverse

The trust deficit scales with solution size for a compound reason.

Small solutions are near-testable in advance. Someone can point to a similar thing done elsewhere.
Large solutions are systemic, which means their full effect only becomes visible after implementation.

And human cognition doesn't handle "trust me, it'll work once fully deployed" well, because it can't.
That's not irrationality, it's epistemic caution under uncertainty. The problem is that epistemic caution under uncertainty is exactly what large problems require you to override.

So the political cost of a real solution is always higher than the political cost of a diluted one, even when the actual cost of the diluted one (in money, in time, in ongoing damage) is far higher.

Functionally, electorates are paying a premium to defer the thing that actually solves the problem.

---

## Why This Isn't Irrationality

The standard framing is that voters are irrational for preferring incremental solutions.
I mean, that's wrong.

Voters are applying a correct prior: novel solutions have no track record, therefore their trust prior is zero.
The problem is that this correct prior is exactly what the problem exploits. Problems grow in the gap between the known tools' capability ceiling and the actual scale of the problem. By the time a real solution is needed, the trust prior for anything sufficient has already been set to zero by definition.

It's not irrationality. It's a structural trap built into how trust works.

---

## The Relationship to Mainstream Epistemology

This maps onto a few traditions without being reducible to any of them.

Peirce's fixation of belief is the closest anchor. Belief gets fixed by habit, authority, a priori reasoning, or method. What this framework adds is a political economy layer: trust is fixed by familiarity, and familiarity requires prior exposure that novel solutions structurally cannot have.

Popper's falsificationism runs in the opposite direction. He says prefer bold conjectures because they're more falsifiable. Electorates do the inverse, they punish bold conjectures precisely because they're untested. Popper is normative about what good epistemology should do. This framework is descriptive about why mass epistemology can't do it.

Kuhn's paradigm shifts map the closest to the dilution cycle. Normal science runs within a paradigm until anomalies accumulate to crisis. Then a revolutionary shift becomes possible. The political version:

diluted solutions = normal science
repeated visible failure = anomaly accumulation
crisis = the moment the novel solution becomes politically viable

The difference is Kuhn's scientists are at least trying to get it right. Political actors are navigating trust thresholds, which is a social phenomenon, not an epistemic one.

Habermas's communicative rationality assumes open discourse can produce legitimate consensus. The "solutions aren't newsworthy" problem is a direct structural refutation of the conditions Habermas required. The media-political environment is adversarially shaped toward problem amplification, not solution legitimation.

Burke actually gets partially vindicated here, not normatively but descriptively. His argument that existing institutions encode accumulated wisdom is wrong as a reason to block solutions, but accurate as a description of why electorates behave that way. The distrust of novelty IS epistemically grounded, it just produces the wrong policy outcome at scale.

---

## What's Distinct

Most philosophy treats the epistemology of solutions as a knowledge problem. Can we know if it works? Is the evidence sufficient?

This framework treats it as a trust dynamics problem with its own feedback loop.

The catch-22 structure -- solutions require novel thought, novel thought is distrusted, distrust is itself a problem compounding the original -- is a self-sealing trap. Mainstream epistemology doesn't model it cleanly because it doesn't account for the social legitimation layer between a solution's validity and its political viability.

The nearest combined frame would be Overton Window theory plus Kuhnian crisis dynamics plus Peirce's fixation of belief. But none of them alone captures the trust-scale inverse relationship, or the dilution-exhaustion mechanism as a necessary precondition for real solutions passing.

That mechanism is the thing worth formalising.
